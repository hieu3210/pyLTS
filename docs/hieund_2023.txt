Abstract: Linguistic time series forecasting model (LTS-FM), which has been recently proposed,
uses linguistic words of linguistic variable domain generated by hedge algebras (HAs) to describe
historical numeric time series data. Then, the LTS-FM is established by utilizing real numeric semantics of words which induced by the fuzziness parameter values (FPVs) of HAs. In the existing
LTS-FMs, just the FPVs of HAs are optimized whilst the used word set is still chosen by human
experts. This paper proposes a co-optimization method of selecting the optimal used word set which
best describes numeric time series data in parallel with choosing best FPVs of HAs to improve the
accuracy of LTS-FM by utilizing particle swarm optimization (PSO). In this co-optimization method,
the outer loop optimizes the FPVs of HAs whilst the inner loop optimizes the used word set. The
experimental results on three datasets of the “enrollments of the University of Alabama” (EUA), the
“killed in car road accidents in Belgium” (CAB), and the “spot gold in Turkey” (SGT) show that our
proposed forecasting model outperforms the existing forecasting models in terms of forecasted accuracy.

The human ability to predict future events and phenomena has attracted the scientific community for many years with a bunch of proposed forecasting methodologies
based on observed historical data. In particular, the forecasting method based on time
series analysis has been investigated by numerous researchers with various models such
as ARMA, ARIMA, and so on. In time series forecasting models, future values can be
forecasted based on only past data. The characteristics of the time series data such as
trends, seasonality, stability, outliers, etc. have been considered to establish forecasting
models for future values.
The fuzzy time series proposed by Q. Song & B. S. Chissom [1-3] is a forecasting
method of time series analysis that combines the principles of the fuzzy set theory proposed by L. A. Zadeh [4] with traditional time series techniques. Fuzzy time series is particularly useful in handling the uncertainty, vagueness, and imprecision of data, which is

variables as fuzzy sets rather than crisp sets. In fuzzy time series, the forecasted values are
not simply single point estimates, but rather a range of values that reflect the uncertainty

People often use natural language as a tool to communicate effectively with each
other. They also store and process recorded information through linguistic variables with
their values. Linguistic values are also used by humans to forecast events occurring in
nature and society so that they can better prepare for future planning. Beside fuzzy set
theory for handling linguistic variables, hedge algebras (HAs) proposed by N. C. Ho and
W. Wechler [9, 10] exploits the order-based semantics structure of word domains of linguistic variables which provides a mathematical formalism for generating computational
semantics of words that can be applied to solve the real-life application problems in various domains such as image processing [11], fuzzy control [12], data mining [13–17], etc.
HAs is the qualitative model, therefore, it needs to be quantified by measurable quantities
based on qualitative semantics. The words of linguistic variables convey their qualitative
semantics in relationship with the other words in the whole variable domain. Because the
linguistic words of a variable are interdependent, they only disclose their full qualitative
semantics in the comparative context of the whole variable domain. The fuzziness measure of linguistic words [18] is the crucial concept of fuzzy information and plays an important role in hedge algebras quantification. The quantitative or numerical semantics of
a word induced by the fuzziness measures of words is the semantically quantifying mapping (SQM) value that is the basis for generating the computational semantics for solving
real-world application problems. Recently, hedge algebras have been applied to solve the
time series forecasting problem in such a way that the historical numeric time series data
is transformed into linguistic one by using the real numeric semantics of words defined
based on their corresponding SQM values. Then, the LTS-FMs are established [19–22].
In the LTS-FMs, instead of partitioning the historical data into intervals, a datum of
historical data is assigned a linguistic word based on the nearest real numeric semantic.
By doing so for all historical data, the numeric time series is transformed into LTS. The
linguistic words used to describe historical data are used to define the logical relationship
between the data of the current year and the next year. Then, the linguistic logical relationships (LLRs) and linguistic logical relationship groups (LLRGs) are established. The
fuzzy forecasted values are induced and finally based on them, the corresponding crisp
forecasted values are computed. We can see that the SQM values of words determined by
the FPVs of HAs are crucial in establishing the LTS-FMs. The FPVs of HAs are automatically optimized by PSO in [23]. However, the word set used to describe numeric time
series data is chosen by human experts, so the selected words may not reflect the nature
of the historical data. To overcome this drawback, this paper presents a method of selecting an optimal word set which best describe numeric time series data in parallel with
optimizing the FPVs of HAs by applying a co-optimization algorithm of particle swarm
optimization (Co-PSO). In Co-PSO, the outer loop optimizes the FPVs of HAs whilst the
inner loop optimizes the used word set. The experimental results on three datasets of the
“enrollments of the University of Alabama” (EUA), the “killed in car road accidents in
Belgium” (CAB), and the “spot gold in Turkey” (SGT) show that our proposed forecasting
model has better forecasting accuracy than the existing ones.

Each linguistic domain of variable 𝒳, denoted by Dom(𝒳), consists of a word set that
can be generated from two generator words, e.g., “cold” and “hot” by acting linguistic
hedges on them. For example, with two hedges of “very” and “rather”, the words generated by acting two those hedges on two generator words of “cold” and “hot” can be “very
cold”, “rather cold”, “very hot”, “very very hot”, and so on. We can observe that they are
linearly ordered and are comparable, e.g., “very cold” ≤ “cold” ≤ “rather cold” ≤ “hot” ≤ “very
hot” ≤ “very very hot”.

2.2. Linguistic time series forecasting model

158

Based on the theory of hedge algebras, Hieu et al. in [19] introduced the concept of
LTS and its application to the enrollment forecasting problem. In which, the quantitative
(numerical) semantics, known as SQM values, of linguistic words are directly used to establish the LTS-FM. Specifically, the numerical semantics of words are linearly transformed to the real numerical semantic domain of the universe of discourse (UD) of the
linguistic variable. Thus, each datum of a time series, whether recorded in numerical or
linguistic values, can be natural associated with the corresponding real numerical semantics of the used linguistic words of the LTS. This is also the distinguished and outstanding
properties of LTS compared with fuzzy time series.
In order to have a theoretical basis in proposing our forecasting model, we briefly
present in this subsection some concepts of LTS proposed by Hieu et al. [19].
Definition 1. [19] (LTS) Let 𝕏 be a set of natural linguistic words of a variable 𝒳 defined
on the UD to describe its numeric values. Then, any chronological series L(t), t = 0, 1, 2, …,
where L(t) is a finite subset of 𝕏, is called a linguistic time series.
Definition 2. [19] (LLR) Suppose Xi and Xj are the linguistic words representing the data
at the time t and t+1, respectively. Then, there exists a relationship between Xi and Xj called
a linguistic logical relationship (LLR) and denoted by

3. Improve the LTS-FM by co-optimization of PSO

235

In this subsection, a hybrid LTS-FM integrated with particle swarm co-optimization
(COLTS) is presented. In the LTS-FMs, the word set in the domain of a linguistic variable
is generated automatically by hedge algebras associated with a linguistic variable. Therefore, the cardinality of the generated word set is unlimited. However, in a specific period,
a given number of words are selected to describe the numeric time series. The number of
automatically generated words depends on the max specificity level k of words specified
by human experts and the maximum word length in the word domain is equal to k.
In the proposed optimization model of LTS presented in [23], the word set used to
describe the historical numeric time series data (so-called used word set) should be selected intuitively by human experts before the fuzziness parameter value optimization
processes. Hence, the used word set depends on the human expert’s intuition and may
not be optimal. To select the optimal used word set to best describe historical numeric
time series data from the linguistic variable domain, an optimization process should be
executed. Besides, the FPVs should be optimized in parallel with the word-set selection to
select the best numerical semantics of used words. Therefore, a co-optimization process
for concurrently selecting the optimal used word set and FPVs is applied by utilizing PSO.
Specifically, the co-optimization process includes inner and outer loops. The inner loop
(inner PSO) optimizes the used word set while the outer loop (outer PSO) optimizes FPVs.
Real encoding is used for both outer and inner loop. Specifically, in our experiments, only
two linguistic hedges (Little and Very) are used to generate the word set of the linguistic
variable domain, so the number of the optimized FPVs is only 2. They are the fuzziness
measure of one of two generator words (e.g., fm(c-)) and the fuzziness measure of one of
two hedges (e.g., 𝜇(𝐿𝑖𝑡𝑡𝑙𝑒)). We have fm(c-) + fm(c+) = 1 and 𝜇(𝐿𝑖𝑡𝑡𝑙𝑒) + 𝜇(𝑉𝑒𝑟𝑦) = 1, so

The experiments aim at showing the necessity of optimizing the used linguistic word
set used to describe numeric time series in parallel with optimizing FPVs and our proposed forecasting models outperform the compared ones. To show the efficiency of our
proposed models, their experimental results on the three forecasting problems of the EUA,
CAB, and GT are evaluated and compared with the ones proposed by Uslu et al. [6], Chen
et al. [8], and Phong et al. [23]. The objective function of the optimization problem is the
MSE. The values of MSE, RMSE, and MAPE values are used to evaluate the compared
forecasting models.
In the experiments, our proposed forecasting models were implemented by C# programming language running on Microsoft Windows 10 64-bit with the hardware configuration of an Intel Core i5-8250U 1.6GHz CPU integrated with 8 GB of RAM. Each experiment has been attempted five times. Then, the smallest obtained MSE values are chosen
as the forecasting accuracy.
For the inner iteration, the number of cycles is 100 and the number of particles is 30.
For the outer iteration, the number of cycles is 30 and the number of particles is 20. For
both inner and outer iterations, the Inertia weight ω is 0.4, both the self-cognitive factor c1
and the social cognitive factor c2 are 2.0.
